Read the following passage and answer the questions:

Education has been a topic of philosophical inquiry since ancient times. Some of the earliest important educational thinkers include Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. Socrates, the teacher of Plato, believed in the importance of dialogue and questioning to stimulate critical thinking and the search for truth. He famously claimed that he was not a teacher but a midwife, helping others give birth to their own knowledge. On the other hand, Aristotle, a student of Plato, focused on the practical aspects of education and the cultivation of good habits. He believed that education should aim at producing good and virtuous citizens. Aristotle considered human nature, habit, and reason to be equally important forces to be cultivated in education. During the Medieval period, the idea of Perennialism was first formulated by St. Thomas Aquinas. Perennialism holds that one should teach principles and reasoning of everlasting importance, rather than just facts that are apt to change over time. It emphasizes the development of the human intellect and moral character. Perennialism has both religious and secular forms. In the Renaissance, the French skeptic Michel de Montaigne critically examined education. He questioned the conventional wisdom of his time, challenging the assumption that university-educated philosophers were necessarily wiser than uneducated workers. Montaigne called for an education that focused on individual needs and experiences, emphasizing the importance of self-knowledge and self-improvement. These early educational thinkers laid the groundwork for the theories and approaches that continue to influence education today. The philosophical debates they sparked regarding the purpose, methods, and content of education are still relevant as we strive to create effective and meaningful educational experiences for learners.
